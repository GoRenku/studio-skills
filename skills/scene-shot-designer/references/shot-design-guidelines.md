# Shot Design Guidelines

Design shots around dramatic function first. A good shot list gives generation and editing enough coverage to tell the scene without inventing new story beats.

## Beat First

Split shots around changes in objective, power, reveal, emotion, or information. Do not split mechanically at every line of dialogue.

Useful beat questions:

- What changes for the viewer in this moment?
- Who has power before and after the shot?
- What should the viewer notice first?
- Is the scene clearer as a long take, controlled tableau, or broken coverage?

## Coverage As Options

Include only coverage that serves the scene:

- establishing geography when orientation matters;
- primary action coverage;
- dialogue coverage when faces, listening, and timing matter;
- reaction shots for decisions, wounds, lies, or withheld information;
- inserts for props, hands, evidence, devices, thresholds, or documents;
- transitions when a visual handoff matters.

Avoid bloated shot counts. If one controlled master carries the scene better, use it.

## Audience Attention

Each shot should name what the viewer should watch: a face, hand, prop, doorway, silence, status shift, threat, contradiction, or absence.

## Dialogue And Reaction

Dialogue shots should explain who speaks, who listens, and which reaction matters. Not every line needs its own shot. Covered block indexes should still make the intended screenplay coverage clear.

## Screen Direction And Geography

Maintain eyelines, crossing behavior, subject position, and location orientation unless breaking them is an intentional creative choice. If a scene benefits from disorientation, name that intent.

## Translate Visual References

When the user names a filmmaker, film, photographer, painter, or visual reference, translate it into mechanics:

- framing and subject placement;
- symmetry or imbalance;
- movement style;
- camera distance;
- lens feel;
- palette and separation;
- lighting behavior;
- texture and surface;
- blocking and rhythm.

Avoid unsupported claims. Say what the reference suggests for this project instead of pretending to know private influence.

## Aspect Ratio

Use the project aspect ratio by default. Add shot-level `aspectRatio` only for a deliberate effect, such as a square insert, surveillance frame, memory fragment, or stylized rupture.

## Audio Notes

Use audio notes for story-relevant sound: room tone, silence, offscreen action, voiceover emphasis, overlapping dialogue, or a sound bridge. Do not add crew logistics.
